# Source: Letter to The Black Dog Institute

**Author:** [[Ashriel]]
**Recipient:** [[Black Dog Institute]]

## Summary
A piece of formal correspondence sent by developer Ashriel to the Black Dog Institute (an Australian mental health research organization) critiquing the methodological design of their survey on AI and mental health. 

## Key Arguments & Sociological Critiques

### 1. Confounding Variables in Neurodivergence
Ashriel highlights that questions measuring "reduced social behavior" or "decreased willingness to seek professional help" risk producing wildly misleading correlations. Because [[AuDHD]] individuals baseline with different social engagement patterns and experience high friction in traditional therapy, the survey is likely to falsely attribute these pre-existing neurodivergent traits to "harm caused by AI."

### 2. Dismantling "The Therapy Hierarchy"
Ashriel challenges the institutional assumption that "professional support = gold standard" and "AI = concerning substitute." Drawing on 25 years of lived experience, Ashriel notes that human therapists are frequently poorly equipped for autistic communication styles. Therefore, a respondent indicating they are "less likely to seek professional help" after using AI is not experiencing digital isolation—they have simply found a tool that works better than the failing human baseline. 

## Technical Framing
To legitimize the critique, Ashriel leverages their extensive professional background in full-stack machine learning pipelines and citizen science contributions to [[Anthropic]], positioning themselves as an expert bridging the gap between lived socio-technical experience and raw AI architecture.
