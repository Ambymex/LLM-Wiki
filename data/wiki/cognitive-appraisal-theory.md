# Cognitive Appraisal Theory

Cognitive Appraisal Theory is a psychological framework asserting that emotions are extracted from our evaluations (appraisals) of events and environmental stimuli, rather than being simple reflex reactions.

In standard appraisal models, emotions emerge from dimensional evaluations such as:
- **Pleasantness**: Is the situation good or bad?
- **Self-Agency vs. Other-Agency**: Who caused the situation?
- **Predictability / Suddenness**: Was the event expected?

For example, an unpleasant situation caused by oneself generates *guilt*, whereas an unpleasant situation caused by external, uncontrollable factors generates *sadness*.

## Application in Artificial Intelligence
Recent work in [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] has revealed that [[Large Language Models]] inherently map human emotional logic onto latent networks that mirror Cognitive Appraisal Theory. When predicting or inferring [[Functional Emotions]], LLMs seem to compute implicit scores for these appraisal dimensions in their mid-layers.

By conducting generation steering and causal interventions (like [[Activation Patching]]), researchers have proven that artificially modulating an LLM's internal representation of an appraisal concept dynamically changes its emotional output. Dialing up "self-agency" in an LLM's hidden state, for instance, pushes its inference from "sadness" to "guilt" in an ecologically valid way.

This structural alignment bridges artificial NLP mechanics with cognitive science, providing tools to evaluate the depth of [[Emulation Layer (Cognitive)]] and the nature of simulated emotion.
