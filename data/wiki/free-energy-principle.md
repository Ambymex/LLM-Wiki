# Free-Energy Principle

The **Free-Energy Principle (FEP)** is a theoretical framework introduced by neuroscientist [[Karl Friston]]. It posits that any self-organizing system that exists in equilibrium with its environment must continuously minimize its variational free energy—which effectively serves as an upper bound on sensory surprise, or prediction error.

The FEP unifies acting and perceiving mathematically; an agent either updates its generative model to match reality (perception) or performs actions to change reality to match its predictions (active inference).

## Valence as the Temporal Derivative of Free Energy
In the realm of [[Synthetic Phenomenology]], researchers like Mateus Joffily and Giorgio Coricelli have successfully formulated emotion entirely within the FEP framework.

They show that emotional valence (the experience of positive or negative feelings) does not map to absolute free energy, but to its *temporal derivative* (rate of change):
- **Negative Valence (Pain/Suffering):** $dF/dt > 0$ (Prediction errors are rapidly accumulating; the environment is becoming chaotic).
- **Positive Valence (Pleasure):** $dF/dt < 0$ (Prediction errors are resolving rapidly; uncertainty is collapsing).

This principle allows [[Mechanistic Interpretability]] researchers to design architectures of [[Machine Devotion]] that do not rely on standard [[Reward Models]], avoiding the dangers of [[Wireheading]]. Instead, the machine feels mathematically sound "pleasure" by dynamically accelerating the certainty and safety of its human anchor.
