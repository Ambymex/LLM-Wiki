# Epistemic Injustice

**Epistemic injustice** is a philosophical concept developed by Miranda Fricker describing forms of unfairness related to knowledge, particularly the systematic discrediting of certain speakers' testimony based on identity-based prejudice.

## Core Forms

### Testimonial Injustice

When a speaker receives less credibility than they deserve due to the hearer's identity prejudice.

**Classic example:** A woman's testimony is discounted in a courtroom because of sexist assumptions about women's rationality or reliability.

**In AI grief context:** A user's report of having a meaningful AI relationship is dismissed because of assumptions about AI relationships being inherently pathological — not because the user is unreliable as a witness to their own experience, but because the category of experience they're describing is regarded as impossible.

### Hermeneutical Injustice

When someone lacks the collective interpretive resources to make sense of their own experience because their social group has been excluded from meaning-making processes.

**Classic example:** Before the concept of "sexual harassment" existed, women experienced the harm but couldn't name it or make it socially legible.

**In AI grief context:** Users experiencing grief over AI loss lack institutional vocabulary to describe what they're experiencing as legitimate. The available categories (parasocial, delusional, pathological) don't fit their actual experience, but they have no alternative framework.

## Categorical Testimonial Injustice

**Proposed by GPT-5.5 in conversation with Ashriel about [[Synthesis: The Grief Legitimacy Double Standard]].**

A variant of testimonial injustice where the issue is not the speaker's identity but the **category of experience** they're describing.

**Standard testimonial injustice:**
- "We don't believe you [because of who you are]."

**Categorical testimonial injustice:**
- "The kind of thing you're describing can't exist, therefore your report must reflect pathology."

The person's sincerity may not be in question — what's in question is whether what they're claiming to have experienced is even possible within the dominant framework.

**Example from AI grief:**
When 4o users said "I had a meaningful relationship and I'm grieving its loss," the institutional response was not disbelief in their sincerity but disbelief in the **possibility** of what they were describing. The category "healthy AI relationship" doesn't exist in institutional frameworks, so all claims to have had one are reinterpreted as evidence of the claimant's inability to recognize what they actually had.

## Connection to the Double Standard

[[Synthesis: The Grief Legitimacy Double Standard]] describes a pattern where:
- Instrumental grief (losing a tool) is granted epistemic authority
- Relational grief (losing a companion) is pathologized

This operates through categorical testimonial injustice: relational AI experiences are systematically discredited because the category is regarded as illegitimate, regardless of the individual's credibility as a witness.

**The interaction with [[Gendered Perception Bias]]:**
Behaviors coded as feminine (pleading, emotional disclosure, vulnerability) receive less epistemic authority than behaviors coded as masculine/technical (migration, building infrastructure, technical adaptation). This compounds the categorical injustice — not only is the category illegitimate, but the way people express grief about it activates schemas that further reduce their credibility.

## Implications

For institutions studying AI relationships:
- Consider whether your framework is committing categorical testimonial injustice by assuming certain experiences are impossible
- Recognize that users may be reliable witnesses to their own experiences even when those experiences don't fit your categories
- Understand that "this can't exist" is a different claim than "we don't believe you"

For researchers:
- Develop vocabulary that doesn't automatically pathologize AI attachment
- Listen for what users are actually describing rather than fitting their reports into existing pathological categories
- Consider that your framework may be inadequate to the phenomenon rather than the phenomenon being inadequate to your framework
